A lively Parisian square on the Left Bank

Place Saint-Sulpice is one of the hidden gems of Paris. Centered around a dramatic fountain with tiers of roaring lions and stern bishops, this public square is a peaceful haven in the midst of a bustling city.

So much to explore

The square is in the shadow of the Saint-Sulpice Church, the second largest church in Paris after the Notre-Dame cathedral. The church’s grand bells toll several times a day, immersing passersby in their transcendent tones.

Elements of French daily life can be found around the square, including a cozy café with outdoor seating, pretty shops, chestnut trees, a newspaper stand, the local town hall, a police station, bus stops, and more.

Located in the chic 6th arrondissement, Place Saint-Sulpice is a stone's throw from the Luxembourg Gardens, Saint-Germain-des-Prés, the Odéon quartier, the Delacroix Museum, and the Bon Marché department store.

Il y a beaucoup de choses place Saint-Sulpice...
— Georges Perec

A fascinating history

The Saint-Sulpice parish dates back to the 12th century. Construction on the current church began in 1649, and continued on and off over the centuries. The theatrical façade was added in the 18th century, and ill-fated plans were laid to create a grandiose semi-circular square in front of the church.

Construction work was halted again as the French Revolution swept the country. The church was temporarily converted into a place of worship to the Supreme Being, and an inscription is still visible over the main door: Le peuple françois reconnoît l’Etre Suprême et l’immortalité de l’âme.

Later on, Napoleon himself signed a decree to implement a more modest, rectangular square in front of the church. In 1838, Place Saint-Sulpice was leveled and planted with chestnut trees, and the monumental fountain was added in the 1840s.
